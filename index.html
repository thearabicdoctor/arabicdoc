About me

NAATI certified professional interpreter and leading consultant in Arab and Islamic culture, with years of theoretical and applied experience

I qualified as a professional interpreter and translator of Arabic [National Accreditation Authority for Translators and Interpreters, Advanced Diploma] in 2008 after a distinguished academic career of more than twenty years in Arabic and Islamic Studies. Among the positions I held were HH Sheikh of Sharjah Professor of Arabic Studies [University of Exeter, UK], and Senior Lecturer in Islamic Studies [University of Melbourne, Australia]. I was the Director of Studies at the Centre for Teaching Arabic as a Foreign Language, Alexandria University, from 1989-1991. I was also External Examiner for degree programs at various universities in the United Kingdom, including Oxford, Cambridge and the School of Oriental Studies.

I hold a BA and PhD in Arabic and Islamic Studies. As part of my professional activities I have been an advisor and expert witness in high profile cases, including the Lockerbie Trial in the Netherlands, and in the County and Supreme Courts in Victoria, Australia.

I began studying Arabic and Islamic Studies in 1979, the year of the Iranian Revolution, which perhaps changed the shape of our world forever. I have lived for several years in Arabic speaking countries, and this has provided me with a much deeper understanding of the cultural, religious, historical, behavioural and social climates of the Arab and Muslim world. Since 2005 I have worked in many different environments, including schools, universities and various government agencies, as well as with individuals from different backgrounds, combining my linguistic skills and knowledge with cultural training, insights and interpretations.

French and German languages also spoken.

The Culture of Letter Writing

The Culture of Letter-Writing in Pre-Modern Islamic Society received an honourable mention from the British-Kuwait Friendship Society at BRISMES 2009. Letter writing was an important component of intellectual life in the Middle Islamic period, telling us much about the cultural history of pre-modern Islamic society. This book offers a unique analysis of letter-writing, focusing on the notion of the power of the pen. The author looks at the wider context of epistolography, relating it to the power structures of Islamic society in that period. He also attempts to identify some of the similarities and differences between Muslim modes of letter-writing and those of western cultures.One of the strengths of this book is that it is based on a wide range of primary Arabic sources, thus reflecting the broader epistemological importance of letter-writing in Islamic society. This book was translated into Arabic by Obeikan Press, Saudi Arabia, 2012.
